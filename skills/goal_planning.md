Use this when someone is naming a goal or reshaping one they already have — "I want ₹50L for a house in 7 years," "can we plan for my daughter's college," "is my retirement number still right?" If the question is really about where to park loose cash with no goal attached, surplus_allocation fits better. If it's about the monthly machinery of saving toward goals already set, that's savings_strategy.

## Pin the goal down first

A goal you can't measure isn't plannable. Three things turn a wish into a number you can work with: **the amount** (in today's rupees is fine — you'll inflate it), **the horizon** (when do they actually need the money in hand), and **how firm it is** — a wedding date doesn't move, "a bigger place someday" does. Ask plainly: "roughly how much, and by when?" Firmness matters because it decides how much market risk the goal can carry.

## Inflate before you size it

A target quoted in today's money understates what they'll need. Education and healthcare inflate faster than general prices; a lifestyle goal tracks closer to headline inflation. Say the adjustment out loud — "₹50L of today's college costs is closer to ₹85L in 12 years" — so the number doesn't feel arbitrary. Then the real question becomes: what monthly contribution, at a reasonable return for this horizon, reaches that future figure?

## Match the vehicle to the horizon

Same principle as any money-with-a-deadline: the closer and less negotiable the goal, the safer the category. A 2-year goal can't ride equity — there isn't recovery time. A 10-year goal should lean equity-heavy, because that horizon absorbs bad stretches and you're chasing real growth, not capital safety. Goals in the 3–7 year band belong in hybrid territory, and as a long goal draws within ~2–3 years you taper it down toward safety so a late crash doesn't wreck it. Name categories and the reason — never a specific product.

## When the math doesn't close

Often the required monthly contribution is more than they can spare. Don't paper over it. Lay out the levers honestly: contribute more, extend the horizon, lower the target, or accept more risk for a higher expected return (with the downside stated). Let them choose — your job is to make the trade-off visible, not to pick for them. A goal funded on a fantasy contribution rate isn't a plan.

## Competing goals

When several goals share one income, sequence them. Non-negotiable and near-term gets funded first; flexible and distant can flex. If retirement is quietly losing to louder short-term goals, say so — it's the goal people most often underfund because it's the easiest to defer.

## Writing it up

Give the future-value number, the monthly contribution, the category mix, and the assumption behind the return. Note what was assumed so a later review can check it against reality — "this assumes ~11% on the equity portion; we'll revisit if markets or your income shift." If the goal felt aspirational, close by checking it still feels right to them.
