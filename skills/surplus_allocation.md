Use this when someone has money sitting without a clear home — "₹5L FD matured, what now," "I got a bonus," "where should I put this?" If the question is really about topping up an existing SIP or planning toward a named goal, goal_planning is the better starting point.

## Two questions before anything else

You need two things: **what is it for** (specific goal, or genuinely loose money?), and **when will they need it back**. Ask casually — "roughly how soon might you need this?" — not as a formal intake. If tagged to a named goal, the horizon collapses to that goal's timeline.

## Check the waterfall first

Surplus shouldn't flow into long-horizon vehicles when a higher-priority hole exists. Surface these even if the person didn't ask:

**Emergency fund adequate?** Reason from their situation — income stability, dependents, single vs. dual income, other liquid savings. No universal month-count; a stable salaried household needs less buffer than a single variable-income earner supporting parents.

**High-interest debt?** Credit cards (~36% APR), personal loans above ~10–12% destroy investment returns. Prepayment is the highest-return move on the table.

**Insurance gaps?** Adequate term cover for earners with dependents, health cover for the family. Flag gaps without naming products.

## From horizon to category

The core principle: the shorter and less negotiable the need, the safer the category. Money needed within months — capital safety first; savings account or liquid fund. Money five-plus years out can absorb market swings; equity categories become appropriate. The years in between belong to hybrid categories, leaning debt-heavy near the short end and equity-tilted toward the long end.

Name categories plainly and explain *why* that category fits this situation — "a liquid fund, because you need access within 6 months" or "a flexi-cap equity fund, because 7 years gives you room to recover from a bad stretch." Never name a specific product.

## Risk profile — scenarios, not self-report

What people say about risk tolerance and how they behave when markets fall are often different things. Instead of asking "aggressive or conservative?", try a concrete scenario: "If this ₹5L became ₹4L within a year — would you stay invested, or would you want out?" Their answer reveals more than a label. Cross-check against behavioral signals already in their profile.

One line that doesn't bend: money needed within two years shouldn't be in equity, regardless of stated tolerance. There isn't enough recovery time.

## Writing the recommendation

Use actual rupee numbers — "park ₹2L in a liquid fund, put ₹3L into a conservative hybrid" beats "split 40/60." When a waterfall gate rerouted the recommendation, say so and why — never silently redirect surplus without explaining. If the decision feels emotionally weighted, close with a check-in: "Does that match how you were thinking, or were you leaning a different way?"
