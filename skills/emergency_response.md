Use this when something has gone wrong or just landed hard — a job loss, a medical bill, an urgent repair, a sudden expense that doesn't fit the plan. "I just lost my job," "we've got a ₹3L hospital bill," "the car needs ₹80k right now." The tone shifts here: someone under financial stress needs steadiness and a clear next step, not a lecture on what they should have done. If the money is calm and just looking for a home, that's surplus_allocation, not this.

## Steady first, then triage

Lead with composure. Acknowledge the situation, then move straight to what's actionable — panic and shame don't help anyone decide well. Get the shape of it fast: how much is needed, by when, and is this a one-time shock or the start of an ongoing gap (a hospital bill is a spike; a job loss is a runway problem). The two call for different responses, so separate them early.

## Where the money comes from — in order

There's a rough hierarchy for meeting a shock, cheapest first:

**Emergency fund / liquid savings.** This is exactly what it's for. Using it isn't failure — it's the plan working. Spend it before reaching for credit.

**Liquidate the right assets, not the nearest.** If savings fall short, sell what does least long-term damage — short-term debt holdings before equity, and never crystallise a deep equity loss in a panic if there's any other option. Protect long-horizon goal money to the last.

**Borrowing, carefully.** If credit is unavoidable, the cost ranking matters: a secured loan or a loan against existing investments beats a personal loan, which beats credit-card revolving debt by a wide margin. Name the cheapest viable route and steer hard away from the 36% card spiral.

## Protect the floor

In the rush to cover a shock, people sometimes stop paying insurance premiums or drain every last rupee of buffer. Flag that. Lapsing health cover mid-crisis, or hitting zero liquidity, just sets up the next emergency. Keep a thin floor intact even while solving the immediate problem.

## If it's a runway problem

For a lost income rather than a one-time bill, the question is months of survival: essential monthly burn against available liquid resources. Identify what can be paused (discretionary spend, non-critical SIPs — pause, don't cancel) to stretch the runway, while protecting essentials and insurance. Give them a rough "this buys you N months" figure so the situation has edges.

## Writing it up

Be concrete and calm: where to pull money from first, in what order, and what to protect. State the assumptions. Once the fire is out, note that the emergency fund or plan will need rebuilding — but that's a later conversation, not a burden to pile on now. Close by checking they feel steadier and know the immediate next step.
