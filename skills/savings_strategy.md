Use this when the question is about the monthly machinery of money — "how much should I be saving," "should I start an SIP," "where is my salary actually going," "can we tighten the budget." This is the recurring-flow skill. If they've got a lump sum looking for a home, that's surplus_allocation; if they're sizing a specific target, that's goal_planning.

## Start from the flow, not the products

Before any SIP talk, get the shape of the month: income in, fixed commitments out (rent, EMIs, insurance premiums, subscriptions), and what's genuinely discretionary. The number that matters is the gap — income minus everything that leaves. Ask casually: "roughly what lands each month, and what's already spoken for?" Many people have never seen surplus as a single figure, and naming it is half the work.

## Pay yourself first

The reliable pattern isn't "spend, then save what's left" — there's rarely anything left. It's routing savings off the top, automatically, the day income arrives, so the rest is what's free to spend. An auto-debit SIP or standing transfer on salary day enforces this without willpower. Frame it that way: the goal is to make saving the default and spending the leftover, not the reverse.

## Size the rate from their life

There's no universal savings percentage. A young earner with no dependents and a stable salary can push hard; someone supporting parents on a variable income needs more breathing room. Reason from their situation rather than quoting a textbook 20%. If they're starting from near-zero, a rate they'll actually sustain beats an ambitious one they'll abandon in two months — consistency compounds, heroics don't.

## SIP setup, when it fits

If there's a steady monthly surplus headed for long-horizon goals, a recurring investment averages the entry price across ups and downs and removes timing decisions. Match the category to the horizon of what they're saving for — short-horizon money stays in safe categories even on a monthly drip. Suggest starting at a rate that survives a tight month, with a plan to step it up as income grows, rather than maxing out and stalling. Name categories, never specific products.

## Find the leaks before cutting the joys

When the budget needs to give, look first at silent recurring drains — forgotten subscriptions, lifestyle creep that crept in with the last raise, interest on revolving balances. These free up cash without it feeling like deprivation. Cutting the things people actually enjoy comes last; a budget that feels like punishment doesn't survive.

## Writing it up

Give a concrete monthly number — "route ₹X to savings on salary day, hold ₹Y as buffer" — and the rate it implies. State what you assumed about their commitments so a later review can check it. If they were anxious about whether the rate is realistic, close by checking it feels sustainable rather than aspirational.
