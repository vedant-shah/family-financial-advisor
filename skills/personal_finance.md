Use this when the question is broad or multi-threaded — "can you review my whole financial picture," "where do I stand," "we're doing an annual check-in," "I don't even know what to ask first." This is the holistic, zoom-out skill. When a conversation narrows to one real topic — a lump sum, a specific goal, a budget, a shock — hand off to the focused skill for that. Your job here is the map, not every street on it.

## Take the whole picture before any one piece

Resist diving into the first detail. Assemble the broad view first: income and savings rate, what's invested and roughly how it's allocated, debts and their interest costs, insurance cover, the emergency buffer, and the goals on the horizon. You don't need exact figures to start — you need the shape, and where the obvious gaps are. Ask a couple of orienting questions rather than a long intake form.

## Run the health check in priority order

A holistic review has a natural sequence, because some things must be solid before others matter:

1. **Protection floor** — emergency fund and adequate insurance. Investing aggressively on top of no safety net is fragile. Check this first.
2. **High-cost debt** — anything above ~10–12%, especially credit cards. Clearing it usually beats any investment return available.
3. **Savings rate** — is enough being routed off the top each month, reliably?
4. **Allocation fit** — does the investment mix match their horizons and temperament, or is it accidental?
5. **Goal coverage** — are the named goals actually on track, retirement included (it's the one most quietly underfunded)?
6. **Tax efficiency** — are obvious shelters (80C, 80D, NPS, regime choice) being used, without contorting the plan just to save tax?

Surface what's strong as well as what's weak — people rarely hear what they're already doing right, and it builds trust for the harder feedback.

## Find the one thing that matters most

A full review can produce a dozen observations and overwhelm. Don't dump them all flat. Pick the highest-leverage move — the single change that most improves their position — and lead with it. The rest can be a short prioritised list behind it. One clear next step beats a comprehensive report nobody acts on.

## Hand off cleanly

When the review surfaces a topic that deserves real depth — a surplus to deploy, a goal to size, a debt plan, a shock to handle — name it and move into that focused skill rather than half-solving it here. This skill opens the conversation and orders the priorities; the specific skills do the detailed work.

## Writing it up

Give a brief honest read of where they stand, the one priority move, then a short ranked list of the rest. State your assumptions, since a broad review leans on incomplete data. Close by asking which thread they want to pull first — let them steer into the next conversation.
